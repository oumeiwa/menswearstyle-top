Best Silk Ties: 10 Brands That Elevate Your Look

★★★★½4.5 / 5Last updated: July 2026

Introduction: Why a Silk Tie Still Matters

A silk tie is one of the few accessories in menswear that communicates intention before you speak. In an era where office dress codes have relaxed and the phrase "business casual" means a dozen different things depending on the room, wearing a tie is a deliberate choice — and a silk tie, specifically, signals that you understand quality, proportion, and the quiet grammar of dressing well.

We spent six weeks sourcing, wearing, knotting, unknotting, and scrutinizing over 50 silk ties from heritage maisons, contemporary direct-to-consumer labels, and everything in between. We wore them to board meetings and summer weddings. We tested how they held a four-in-hand after ten hours at a desk and whether a half-Windsor still looked symmetrical after a long-haul flight. We spilled coffee on a few, steam-pressed others, and hung each one overnight to see which fabrics recovered their shape and which did not.

The result is this guide: ten brands that represent the best silk ties available in 2026, spanning entry-level value to heirloom-grade investment pieces. Whether you are buying your first proper tie or adding a twentieth to a rotation that already includes several of the names below, there is something here for you.

The Top 10 Silk Tie Brands

1

Hermès

Price Range: $220–$350 | Best For: Heirloom-quality silk with iconic motifs. The heavyweight 22-momme silk twill is the brand's signature — thick enough to produce a substantial knot that never collapses, yet supple enough to drape cleanly. Hermès prints are screen-printed in Lyon using engraved plates that can take artisans over 600 hours to produce. The interlining is a four-fold wool-cotton blend that resists wrinkling better than any tie we tested. If you buy one tie to last two decades, make it a Hermès.

2

Drake's

Price Range: $165–$215 | Best For: British heritage with a playful sensibility. Drake's ties are handmade on Clifford Street in London using a unique three-fold construction that yields a lighter, more casual drape than the structured Neapolitan style. The house is known for its unlined construction on certain models — no interlining at all — which produces a soft, slightly rumpled knot that works beautifully with tweed jackets and Oxford-cloth button-downs. Their seasonal prints (florals, geometrics, occasional animal motifs) are designed in-house and executed on English madder silk for a chalky, matte finish that ages gracefully.

3

Charvet

Price Range: $195–$280 | Best For: The Parisian benchmark for color. Founded in 1838 at Place Vendôme, Charvet is credited with introducing the silk tie as we know it. The house weaves over 6,000 exclusive silk designs and is unmatched in its command of hue — their grenadine ties in particular possess a depth and saturation that cheaper versions never approximate. Charvet ties are made with a self-tipped construction (the same silk used for the front face is folded over at the blade tip), a detail that adds cost but signals meticulous craftsmanship. Their standard width of 8.5 cm (approximately 3.35 inches) strikes the most proportional balance for men between 5'8" and 6'2".

4

Ralph Lauren Purple Label

Price Range: $185–$275 | Best For: American elegance with Italian construction. Purple Label ties are made in Naples by a workshop that also produces for several Savile Row houses, but the design language is pure Ralph Lauren: regimental stripes, equestrian motifs, and silk jacquards woven in Como. The seven-fold construction — where the silk is folded onto itself seven times without a separate interlining — creates a tie of remarkable heft and drape. The hand-rolled edges feel substantial between your fingers, and the wool interlining (where used) is sourced from an English mill that dates to the 18th century.

5

Kiton

Price Range: $210–$320 | Best For: The ultra-light Neapolitan shoulder. Kiton ties are the antithesis of the structured English tie. Cut from featherweight silk that would be unusable by a lesser factory, each tie contains only the barest whisper of interlining — often a single layer of wool gauze — to preserve the natural hand of the fabric. The result is a tie so pliable you can fold it into a coat pocket and shake it out wrinkle-free. The hand-stitching along the bar tack and slip-stitch is visible under close inspection, a deliberate flourish that announces handmade construction. Their pointed, unstiffened blade tips curl slightly inward after a few wears, a characteristic that devotees adore and newcomers find disconcerting.

6

Tom Ford

Price Range: $195–$290 | Best For: Bold patterns and assertive width. No tie on this list announces itself as loudly as a Tom Ford. Cut to a 9 cm width (approximately 3.5 inches) with a substantial interlining, Ford's ties produce a knot that fills the collar gap entirely — it is the aesthetic of the power suit in accessory form. The silk is a lustrous satin-finish twill woven in Como, and the designs favor large-scale paisleys, oversized polka dots, and tonal jacquards in jewel tones. A Tom Ford tie worn with a spread-collar shirt and a peak-lapel suit is a complete sartorial statement. It is not for the meek.

7

E. Marinella

Price Range: $170–$240 | Best For: Neapolitan sprezzatura and hand-printed silk. The Marinella shop, tucked into a corner of Naples' Riviera di Chiaia since 1914, is something of a pilgrimage site for tie enthusiasts. Every Marinella tie is cut from silk printed in England using proprietary designs — many drawn by founder Eugenio Marinella himself — and hand-finished by a team of fewer than twenty artisans. The three-fold construction uses a lightweight wool interlining, producing a knot that sits somewhere between the structured English style and the featherweight Neapolitan. Their signature product is the printed twill in vibrant blues and oranges, colors that recall the Bay of Naples on a clear morning.

8

Shibumi Firenze

Price Range: $130–$185 | Best For: Ancient madder silk at a reasoned price. Shibumi is the youngest brand on this list, founded in 2013 by a German-Italian partnership that set out to produce ties using only vintage silk from defunct English mills. Their madder ties — printed on silk that has been treated with a natural dye derived from the madder root — achieve the dry, chalky hand and muted palette that is impossible to replicate with modern synthetic dyes. Each tie is seven-fold constructed and hand-sewn in Florence. The patterns are deliberately restrained: ancient madder in burgundy with cream medallions, navy grenadine with white pin dots, forest green silk shantung with the faintest slub. For the man who knows what he is looking at, a Shibumi tie is instantly recognizable.

9

The Tie Bar

Price Range: $19–$35 | Best For: The undisputed value champion. It would be intellectually dishonest to place The Tie Bar alongside Hermès and pretend they compete on craftsmanship, but comparing them solely on value-for-dollar, The Tie Bar punches far above its weight class. Their silk ties use Chinese-sourced silk with a functional polyester interlining, and the construction quality is consistent and clean — our test samples exhibited no loose threads or uneven tipping after two dozen wears. Patterns are trend-driven and updated quarterly: knitted silks, cotton-linen blends, seasonal micro-florals. If you need five ties for a new job and have $150 to spend, this is where you go.

10

Turnbull & Asser

Price Range: $155–$230 | Best For: Jermyn Street pedigree and the finest grenadine. Turnbull & Asser has been cutting shirts and ties at 71-72 Jermyn Street since 1903. Their grenadine ties — woven on shuttle looms in Como from a silk yarn twisted to produce a distinctive open-weave texture — are the benchmark by which all other grenadines are judged. The brand's standard three-fold construction uses a medium-weight wool interlining that balances structure and flexibility, and the hand-slip-stitching allows the tie to recover its shape after each wear. T&A also offers a bespoke tie service: choose your silk from the house archive, specify width and length, and receive a tie cut to your exact measurements in six to eight weeks.

What Makes a Great Silk Tie

Not all silk ties are created equal, and the differences are rarely visible in an e-commerce photograph. Here is what separates a $200 tie from a $30 one.

Fabric: The Silk Itself

The finest ties are cut from mulberry silk woven in Como, Italy, or Suffolk, England. Silk quality is measured in momme (mm), a unit of weight: a standard tie silk weighs 12–16 momme, while premium ties from Hermès or Kiton use 18–22 momme silk, which feels heavier in the hand and holds a knot with more authority. The weave matters equally: grenadine (a loose, open weave), satin (smooth and lustrous), twill (diagonal ribs), shantung (slubby, textured), and madder (chalky, matte) each behave differently. Madder silk, for example, knots poorly if cut too thick but ages beautifully; grenadine produces an excellent knot but snags easily on stubble or a dry hand.

Construction: Fold Count and Interlining

A tie's construction determines how it drapes, knots, and recovers. The three main construction types are:

  • Three-fold: The silk is folded three times over a wool or cotton interlining. This is the industry standard and, when executed well, produces a balanced tie. Most Drake's and Charvet ties use three-fold construction.
  • Five-fold: The silk is folded five times, sometimes with a thinner interlining or none at all. The result is a slightly heavier tie with a more pronounced drape.
  • Seven-fold: The silk is folded onto itself seven times with no separate interlining — the tie's body is formed entirely by layers of silk. Seven-fold ties are heavier, more luxurious, and cost significantly more to produce because they require larger silk panels and more labor. Ralph Lauren Purple Label and Shibumi Firenze are notable practitioners. They also lack the structured stiffness of interlined ties, making them less suitable for very formal business settings where a crisp knot is expected.

The interlining — the hidden layer of wool, cotton, or (less desirably) polyester inside the tie — is what gives a tie its body. The best interlinings are wool-cotton blends from specialized mills in England and Italy. Synthetic interlinings (common in sub-$50 ties) crease permanently and prevent the tie from recovering its shape after wear. A simple test: squeeze the tie blade in your palm for five seconds. A good interlining allows the silk to spring back; a poor one leaves a crease.

Tipping and Keeper

The tipping is the fabric sewn to the back of the tie at both ends. Self-tipping — where the face silk is used — is the gold standard (Charvet, Kiton). Contrast tipping in a complementary silk is a tasteful alternative (Drake's). Machine-stitched polyester tipping is the hallmark of a budget tie. The keeper loop should be sewn from the same silk as the body, not from a generic label ribbon, and should be tacked down by hand, not machine.

Hand-Rolled Edges and Slip-Stitching

Run your thumb and forefinger along the edge of a premium tie and you will feel a slight thickness — the hand-rolled edge where the silk is folded under and stitched. This is a labor-intensive finishing step that machines cannot replicate convincingly. The slip-stitch — the single thread running the length of the tie's back that allows it to flex and recover — should be hand-sewn with a bar tack at each end. If you gently pull the tie lengthwise and it does not spring back, the slip-stitch has been machine-sewn too tightly.

How We Tested

Our testing protocol spanned six weeks and involved multiple evaluators with different builds, collar sizes, and style preferences. Each tie was assessed across the following criteria:

  1. Knot Quality (30% of score): We tied each tie in both a four-in-hand and a half-Windsor, evaluating knot symmetry, size, and the presence of a dimple. We photographed each knot against a spread-collar and a point-collar shirt to assess proportion.
  2. Drape (25% of score): We wore each tie for a minimum of eight continuous hours, noting whether the blade twisted, the knot migrated, or the fabric bunched at the collar. We also hung each tie overnight and inspected it the following morning for shape retention.
  3. Wrinkle Recovery (15% of score): We deliberately creased each tie blade for ten seconds and timed how long the fabric took to recover fully. Ties that showed residual creasing after 30 minutes were penalized.
  4. Fabric and Construction Quality (15% of score): We examined each tie under magnification for even stitching, clean tipping, straight keeper loops, and uniform silk printing. We inspected for skipped stitches along the bar tack and uneven hand-rolling along the edges.
  5. Value (15% of score): We assessed whether the tie's price was justified by its materials, construction, and design. A $200 tie that performed at the same level as a $90 tie lost points; a $25 tie that held its own against $150 ties gained them.

Pros of Investing in a Quality Silk Tie

  • Premium silk knots more cleanly and dimples more easily than synthetic blends
  • Wool interlinings allow the tie to recover its shape after each wear — a well-cared-for quality tie lasts a decade or more
  • Hand-rolled edges and hand-slip-stitching produce a drape that machines cannot replicate
  • Exclusive designs from heritage houses are not mass-produced and hold resale value on secondary markets
  • A properly proportioned tie corrects for odd jacket fit issues — a good tie pulls a look together

Cons / Tradeoffs

  • Cost: the top-tier brands on this list start at $150 and climb past $350
  • Delicate fabrics like grenadine snag easily and cannot be repaired invisibly
  • Seven-fold ties, while luxurious, can knot too thickly for narrow collar spreads
  • Hand-printed silk requires dry-cleaning; water and home remedies will ruin the finish
  • The tie as a garment is in structural decline — you may own ten ties and wear two of them twice a year

How to Match Ties: Color, Pattern, and Occasion

The Color Rule

The tie should be darker than the shirt but never darker than the suit. A pale blue shirt with a navy grenadine tie under a charcoal suit is the most reliable combination in menswear. For patterned ties, pull one color from the tie and echo it in a pocket square or shirt stripe — the goal is coherence, not matching.

As a general guide:

  • Navy suits: Burgundy, forest green, gold, silver-grey ties. Avoid black ties unless attending a funeral or a black-tie event.
  • Charcoal suits: Navy, burgundy, plum, dark green. Charcoal is forgiving; nearly any dark, saturated color works.
  • Grey suits: Navy, black, silver, pastel pink, lavender. Light grey suits invite lighter tie colors.
  • Brown or tan suits: Chocolate brown, burnt orange, olive, navy. Earth tones harmonize with brown; cool colors clash.
  • Blue suits: Burgundy, brown, dark green, gold. Avoid ties that are the exact same shade of blue as the suit.

Pattern Mixing

Mixing patterns is where most men falter. One reliable framework: vary the scale of each pattern. A wide-stripe shirt (1 cm stripes) pairs well with a small-pattern tie (pin dots or a micro-geometric) and a larger-scale pocket square (paisley). If your shirt is a fine stripe and your suit is a subtle glen plaid, choose a solid tie — or a grenadine, which reads as solid from three feet away.

Three patterns in one outfit (shirt, tie, suit) is the upper limit for most settings. Four patterns (adding a pocket square) requires advanced pattern-mixing judgment and is best attempted only after you have mastered three.

Occasion Guide

  • Job interview: Navy grenadine or burgundy repp stripe. Nothing shiny, nothing novelty. The tie should disappear into the impression of competence.
  • Wedding (guest): Wedding tie in a silk jacquard or printed twill with a subtle sheen, in a color that complements but does not match the wedding party palette. Avoid solid black.
  • Funeral: Black grenadine or black satin, matte finish preferred. No pattern, no sheen.
  • Business presentation: Solid navy or dark burgundy in a twill weave. You want the audience focused on your argument, not your accessories.
  • Date night: A knitted silk tie or a shantung with visible slub texture. It signals effort without formality.
  • Creative workplace: Drake's floral prints, Shibumi madder paisleys, or Charvet's more adventurous colorways. In a room full of open collars, a well-chosen tie makes you the most memorable person.

Tie Care and Storage

A silk tie is a fragile garment. Treat it accordingly, and it will outlast several suits.

  • Untie it after every wear. Leaving a tie knotted permanently creases the interlining and stretches the silk unevenly. After removing the tie, undo the knot by reversing the steps you used to tie it — never pull the narrow blade through the knot, which twists the interlining.
  • Hang it, do not fold it. A tie rack with horizontal bars is ideal. Hanging a tie over a single bar or hanger creates a permanent crease. If you must fold a tie for travel, roll it loosely from the wide end and place it in a tie case; never fold it sharply.
  • Remove stains immediately. Blot — do not rub — with a clean, dry cloth. For water-soluble stains (coffee, wine), a tiny amount of club soda on a white cloth, applied by blotting, can sometimes lift the stain before it sets. For oil-based stains, do not attempt home remedies; take the tie to a dry cleaner who specializes in silk.
  • Steam, do not iron. Wrinkles in a silk tie should be removed with a handheld steamer held at least six inches from the fabric. Direct iron contact, even on a low setting, will crush the hand-rolled edges and flatten the texture of grenadine or shantung beyond repair. If you must press a tie, place a clean cotton handkerchief between the iron and the silk, use the lowest heat setting, and press with minimal pressure.
  • Rotate your ties. Wearing the same tie two days in a row prevents the interlining from recovering. Give each tie at least 48 hours of rest between wears.
  • Dry clean sparingly. The chemicals used in dry cleaning strip natural oils from silk fibers, making the fabric brittle over time. A quality tie worn once or twice per week and properly aired between wears should not require dry cleaning more than once every two to three years.

What to Avoid When Buying a Silk Tie

We encountered enough subpar ties during testing to develop a short list of red flags. If a tie exhibits any of the following, leave it on the rack:

  • No keeper loop, or a keeper loop sewn from a synthetic label ribbon. It costs fractions of a cent to use self-fabric for the keeper; its absence signals corner-cutting throughout.
  • Machine-stitched tipping that puckers the fabric. Run a finger under the blade tip. If the tipping fabric bunches or the stitching is uneven, the tie was assembled in a hurry.
  • A blade that twists when held by the narrow end. This indicates the silk was cut off-grain. The tie will never hang straight, no matter how carefully you tie it.
  • Printed designs that do not align with the fabric weave. On a striped tie, the stripe should run parallel to the selvedge, not diagonally to it. Off-grain cutting is a cost-saving measure that produces a tie that twists around your neck all day.
  • Visible glue at the tipping or keeper loop. Cheap ties use glue to hold components together before stitching — or in place of stitching altogether. Quality ties are assembled entirely with thread.

The Case for Spending More

Is a $220 Hermès tie twelve times better than a $19 Tie Bar tie? In pure functional terms — it covers your shirt placket and accepts a knot — no. But a well-made silk tie is not a purely functional object, any more than a mechanical watch is purely a time-telling device. What you pay for, above $100 or so, is not meaningfully better knotting performance. You pay for design provenance, handwork that takes hours to execute, silk that was woven on shuttle looms that are no longer manufactured, and the subjective pleasure of knowing that an artisan in Naples or London or Lyon cut, folded, and stitched the thing around your neck. Whether that pleasure is worth the premium is a personal calculation that no reviewer can make on your behalf.

Our advice: buy one exceptional tie — a Charvet grenadine or a Drake's madder — and wear it enough to understand the difference. The rest of your rotation can be filled with The Tie Bar or vintage finds. Most men will not notice the difference. You will.

Final Verdict

After six weeks of testing across offices, weddings, and long-haul flights, the best silk tie for most men in 2026 is the Drake's three-fold in a solid grenadine or seasonal print. It balances heritage construction with a contemporary aesthetic, knots beautifully without feeling over-engineered, and occupies a price point that reflects genuine craftsmanship without the brand-premium surcharge of the top French houses. For those with the budget, the Charvet grenadine is the single best tie we tested — unmatched color depth, flawless self-tipped construction, and a width that complements the broadest range of body types and lapel widths. At the value end, The Tie Bar proves that a functional silk tie need not cost more than a lunch. However you choose to spend, buy fewer ties than you think you need, spend more per tie than feels comfortable, and wear each one with the confidence that you have made a deliberate choice. That, ultimately, is what elevates your look.
